## [4] PILLAR ANALYSIS

### PILLAR 1 — MONETARY ENVIRONMENT | Score: 3.5 / 10

Let me tell you what the data says aloud, and then what it whispers.

Aloud: Fed Funds Rate at 3.64% remains well above the ~2.5% neutral rate. Real FFR has eased slightly from 1.28% to 1.22%, which reads as mildly less restrictive. The yield curve 10Y−3M sits at 0.56 and 10Y−2Y at 0.51, both positive after re-steepening from their inverted phase. M2 YoY holds at 4.88% — technically expansionary. The surface reading is: monetary conditions are restrictive but not tightening further.

Now the whisper. The reverse repo facility fell from 137M to 82M in seven days. A forty-percent single-week drawdown of the system's liquidity reserve. RRP is the plumbing — when it drains, the broader monetary base quietly loses its buffer. M2 YoY at 4.88% is measuring the stock of money, not the quality of liquidity inside the Treasury market or the banking system. The stock can look healthy while the flow turns. Seven months ago, RRP was above 300M. The trajectory is unambiguous: excess liquidity is being absorbed, not replenished.

Last week I wrote that the yield curve normalization after inversion is historically one of the most dangerous transitions. The configuration this week confirms the bear steepener diagnosis. The 10Y is 4.25% and the 2Y is 3.79% — both elevated, with no meaningful compression on the short end. If this were a bull steepener (Fed cutting aggressively, 2Y falling fast), we would have seen 2Y dropping toward 3%. Instead, 2Y actually ticked up from 3.78% to 3.79%. The long end is doing the work, and that type of steepener does not accompany a Fed-driven relief rally; it accompanies inflation concern, fiscal stress, and term-premium expansion. WTI at $95 — a +13% move since last week — reinforces that inflation optionality.

Real 10Y at 1.83% remains a meaningful discount rate for equity valuations. Each additional basis point of real yield compresses fair value. Combined with CAPE at 40.34x, the math does not improve this week — it only postpones the reckoning.

Score unchanged at 3.5. Mild Real FFR easing is offset by RRP drainage.

### PILLAR 5 — CREDIT CYCLE | Score: 6.0 / 10

Credit remains the stubborn contrarian. HY at 286bp — exactly where it was last week. IG at 80bp — one basis point tighter than last week's 81bp. SLOOS at 5.3% unchanged. CC Delinquency at 2.94% unchanged.

On Marks' pendulum, we are clearly still in Phase 1-2 territory — loose credit conditions, willingness to lend, ample liquidity for corporate borrowers. HY at 286bp is deep inside the risk-on zone. No credit cycle has ever turned without first showing spread widening, and we are not seeing it.

But remember the axiom: the trend of spreads matters more than the absolute level. The fact that spreads are unchanged for two consecutive weeks at an environment where valuation, sentiment, and consumer conditions are all deteriorating is itself information. Credit is pricing no stress. Either credit is right and the equity risks are being overstated by the rest of the pillars, or credit is lagging and the spread widening is being delayed by the carry-trade dynamics that support corporate bond demand. I lean toward the latter because: (1) CC Delinquency at 2.94% has been above the 2.5% watch threshold for multiple weeks, (2) SLOOS at 5.3% shows banks already tightening, and (3) credit spreads have historically lagged equity stress by 3-6 months in modern cycles.

The pendulum is still in Phase 1-2, but the weight is shifting. Phase 3 (restriction/negative impulse) is not this week's story, but the pre-conditions for it are accumulating. Watch HY: a move above 350bp is the first meaningful warning; above 400bp is the regime change I have been describing.

## [6] MARKS CONCLUSION & POSITIONING

### Cyclical position

I said last week that the greatest danger in pricing is not the obvious — it is the configuration where the obvious problem (extreme valuations) is accompanied by a quiet second-order deterioration that most observers will dismiss as noise. This week, two such second-order deteriorations revealed themselves: Consumer Sentiment lost 3.3 points in a single reading, and the Fed's excess liquidity facility shed forty percent of its stock. These are not small events dressed up in technical language — they are structural shifts that, independently, would merit attention; together, they are the pattern of a platform whose supports are being systematically removed while the surface price remains stable.

Marks' rule: you never know exactly where you are in the cycle, but knowing approximately is enough. My approximate position has not changed: we are late cycle, possibly topping. The valuation picture continues to say the same thing it said last week, and for forty consecutive weeks before that. What has changed is the weight of the second-order evidence: the consumer is breaking, the liquidity cushion is thinning, and oil has added a stagflationary optionality that wasn't there seven days ago. Against this, the case for holding offensive positioning has become meaningfully weaker — not because valuation is "more expensive" (it is approximately unchanged) but because the dependencies required for the expensive valuation to remain tenable are eroding.

The right response in this configuration is not to short. It is to hold a defensive portfolio with clearly defined escalation and de-escalation triggers. The cost of protection has eased very slightly: VIX 18.62 and SKEW 139.59 price options modestly cheaper than last week's readings. Use that small gift to add rather than subtract hedge exposure.
